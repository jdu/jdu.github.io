We have a weird problem at Wellcome. We have too much data.

It's sprawling: Wellcome has a wide remit across a large number of domains and undertakings that cross a lot of themes. Contrasted with a product-oriented company, Wellcomes remit can feel frenetic and crazy at times when you're trying to reconcile between different sources and systems at play external, and internal to the organisation.

We have integrations, machine learning activities, "soft" processes that revolve around spreadsheets, and more that all need reconciliation.

With our remit spanning all the domains that we fund, investments, finance, people management, portfolio management, and more. Meaning can get lost, muddied or confused across different teams and departments, and even between two people in the same team.

The Lead-In

We've just come off of a good stretch of work to migrate our data pipelines and infrastructure from a closed proprietary set of platforms to something more modern and flexible. It's reduced our overall cost massively in terms of licensing costs, and opened up access to a lot more flexibility around using the appropriate tools for any given situation and the ability to scale workloads in multiple directions.

All of this while remaining maintainable for a small, very talented team of software engineers.

We've made huge strides in bringing greater volumes of data, and bringing new technical capabilities to our engineering team. That included building custom tooling like our teams `ops-tool` that smooths over some of the warts of setup and teardown of airflow development environments with strong controls around access to data.

And we've made huge reductions in time to deliver data, and controls and cost around that data. But we still have more to do around performance improvements, governance controls, and all the other bits that float around a modern data infrastructure.

Old Problems, Meet New Problems

We've seen significant speed-ups in our teams delivery speed, and more room to focus on the hard problems. But that hasn't necessarily translated up to teams that rely on the outputs of our team. Warehouse tables, data products, integrations and more.

So we need to shift some of our internal focus, outwards, to create tools, systems, and primitives that support Insights & Analytics Teams, ML Engineers, Data Owners, Governance functions, and ultimately the whole org itself to make sure that we can unlock the same speed ups and agility we've unlocked for ourselves, for everyone else.

Doing anything otherwise would be self-defeating.

Enter Semantic Data

Modern data systems (data lakes, parquet files, iceberg, streaming systems, worker-based parallelism) can end up sprawling really easily. We have structures in place to guard against that, but for a long time, governance consisted of documenting our controls with little insight into the actual inner-workings of how we apply and manage it for people like Data Owners.

Semantic Data has opened up a whole world of possibilities on that front that currently require a lot of duct tape. As an smart, highly technical team this is how we plan to tackle it all.

Unified Data Architecture (UDA)

This is not an enterprise data model. What we're doing isn't modelling the data across all the systems and sources that are at play in the organisation. That generally is a bit of a fools errand where you're in a constant cycle of updates to the model to keep up with change in the upstream systems.

For the purposes here, we don't particularly care how data is modeled and managed in the systems upstream from us.
